Active Central Differential Controller

Automotive ECU Logic & Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL) Simulation

Domain Automotive / Motorsport
Logic Inspiration MoTeC SDC (Torque Mapping)
Hardware Arduino, L298N, ADC, I2C
Actuation PWM Axle Speed Distribution

Project Context & R&D Consulting

This project was commissioned for an academic Master's Thesis focused on the theoretical operation of an active central differential in a 4WD vehicle. The client required a physical Hardware-in-the-Loop (HIL) demonstrator to visually and mathematically validate how torque is actively distributed between the front and rear axles during various driving states.

My role was to design the embedded architecture, select the electronic components, and implement the C++ control logic capable of reading simulated driver inputs and dynamically adjusting the drivetrain response in real-time.

Signal Processing & Actuation

To simulate vehicle dynamics safely on a workbench, the demonstrator utilized a 4WD chassis driven by DC geared motors controlled via an L298N dual H-bridge. Torque distribution was physically simulated by dynamically varying the PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) duty cycle delivered to the front vs. rear axle motors.

Analog Data Acquisition

Driver inputs (Throttle Position and Brake Force) were simulated using 10kOhm linear potentiometers. This specific resistance was chosen to strictly comply with the ATMega processor's TTL voltage standards (0-5V), minimizing current draw on the 8-bit ADC pins and preventing signal voltage drops that would corrupt telemetry data.

Real-Time Telemetry (HMI)

A 20x4 LCD (communicating via the I2C bus to save MCU pins) acts as the diagnostic dashboard. It provides the operator with instant numerical feedback regarding throttle percentage, braking force, and the resulting front/rear torque bias calculated by the algorithm.

ECU Logic: Asymmetric Torque Mapping

The true complexity of an active differential lies in its control algorithm. A naive system would simply lock the axles equally. However, real-world vehicle dynamics dictate that a car needs different mechanical characteristics when entering a corner (braking) versus exiting a corner (accelerating).

Motorsport-Inspired Control Strategy

To make the demonstrator behave like a production-grade automotive ECU, I modeled the firmware's logic upon the mapping strategies used in professional motorsport controllers (such as the MoTeC SDC). The C++ algorithm utilizes dynamic lookup tables that evaluate Throttle Position (TP) and Brake Force. Consequently, the calculated differential locking value (and the resulting PWM distribution to the axles) is highly asymmetrical—applying aggressive locking during hard acceleration for maximum traction, while allowing axle slip during braking to maintain steering geometry.
